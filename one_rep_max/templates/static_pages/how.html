  • Estimate a One-Rep-Max Using Computer Vision
    Your one rep max can accurately be computed with just a video. If the bar can be tracked, then pixels can be converted to meters, and velocity can be determined. If velocity is known, the a percent of your one rep max can be established.
How does it work?

  • Upload a stable, head-on video of a proper benchpress
  • Your video will spend some time being processed, and you'll be notified via e-mail once complete
  • The output video will display a scalar multiplier with each rep that represents how much weight you're capable of lifting

Why does it work?

  • The barbell in the uploaded video is tracked using computer vision
  • Olympic barbells have standard dimensions, so the relative height of your repetition can be extracted
  • The average velocity of a repetition can be calculating by using the above height and the time it took for the rep to complete
  • Average velocity is strongly correlated to a percent of a one rep maximum

The Algorithm behind the One Rep Max Calculator

The computations behind the application here are extremely in-depth, but here's the up-front and concise explanation behind it:

  • An uploaded video is analyzed frame by frame to detect an olympic barbell in the image. This in itself can be a fairly intense process, and over time with enough detections we might be able to apply additional machine learning techniques to greatly enhance the algorithm and make it faster.
  • Finding the barbell in each frames gives us two things:
    • Since a standard olympic barbell 2.2 meters, we can convert pixels in the rest of the image to meters because of the known size of the bar. This means that it's possible to determine the distance traveled in every single repetition.
    • A tracked barbell will also give us the height of the bar at every frame in the image. From there it's trivial to determine the starting and stopping points of every repetition based on position differentials.
  • After individual repetitions are identified, we can obtain the bar's velocity by combining the known distance in meters with the time it took to complete the repetition using the video's frame rate.
  • Velocity correlates strongly to one rep max repetitions. Various research papers can be found on that particular subject.

Why was velocity used to obtain a one rep max?

If you're into physics, velocity doesn't seem like the intuitive choice to calculate a max. It's possible for velocity to remain constant even with no force applied. It would seem more intuitive to measure acceleration and obtain force applied and output a one rep max from there. However, trial and error proved that this method doesn't work for a few different reasons:

  • During the performance of any repetition with a weight, the instance acceleration at a given point may be drastically higher than what you're capable of in the rest of the rep. For example, a person pressing 315 lbs may very well apply nearly 400 lbs of force during one portion of the lift.
  • Acceleration doesn't account for sticking points in the lift, which most certainly exist for anyone with bench pressing.
  • You are stronger directly after the eccentric portion of the lift. You will not be as strong during the rest of the lift.
  • In any compound exercise, more than one muscle group is used to complete the lift. So in a bench press, you certainly use your chest, but you also use your lats, shoulders, triceps, and forearms. When you go through the motions of the lift, you transition from one muscle group to the other.
  • Instantaneous acceleration won't account for proper form used during the lift.
  • If you look at a graph of the lift as it's performed, you'll see that the plotted points don't match closely to a quadratic equation at all (it's easy to see that acceleration is not constant throughout the lift). Typically, there's a huge burst of acceleration at the beginning of the lift followed by steady motion through the rest of it. See an example chart of position over time of a bench press set:

The case against acceleration is obvious, but the case for velocity is a lot less obvious. Simply put, I found that a lot of research has already been done on this subject, and using velocity turned out to be the standard way to measure and estimate a one rep max, so we weren't going to try and recreate the wheel. The difference between the research papers and this application, however, is that expensive sensors were used that weren't very commonplace. This application attempts to use the same variables as the researchers, but computer vision is used instead.
