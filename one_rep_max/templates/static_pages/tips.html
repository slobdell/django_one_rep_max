  • Estimate a One-Rep-Max Using Computer Vision
    Your one rep max can accurately be computed with just a video. If the bar can be tracked, then pixels can be converted to meters, and velocity can be determined. If velocity is known, the a percent of your one rep max can be established.
How does it work?

  • Upload a stable, head-on video of a proper benchpress
  • Your video will spend some time being processed, and you'll be notified via e-mail once complete
  • The output video will display a scalar multiplier with each rep that represents how much weight you're capable of lifting

Why does it work?

  • The barbell in the uploaded video is tracked using computer vision
  • Olympic barbells have standard dimensions, so the relative height of your repetition can be extracted
  • The average velocity of a repetition can be calculating by using the above height and the time it took for the rep to complete
  • Average velocity is strongly correlated to a percent of a one rep maximum

Calculating Your One Rep Max Accurately

You may greet this application with immediate skepticism, but the reality is that the algorithm behind the one rep max estimations is extremely sophisticated and works well. However, it's a tool to augment your training and not the end-all be-all of training calculators, and it requires that you as the user provide clean inputs.

You can check out exactly how the calculator estimates your max, but here's all that you really need to know:

  • Use a weight that's heavy enough to be challenging but light enough to lift without needing a spotter.
  • Even though the weight should be relatively light, treat the weight as though it is heavy and lift as hard as possible. A one rep max is being estimated, and if you treat the weight like a warm-up, you'll get calculations accordingly.
  • The video needs to be stable. A shaky video will result in inaccurate starting and ending points relative to one another. This in essence changes the perceived distance the barbell traveled and will therefore give inaccurate readings for velocity which is what the algorithm is based off of (see the explanation of the calculator algorithm for why velocity is used).
  • The video needs to be head on so that the barbell isn't angled in any way from the camera's perspective.
  • Don't use so much weight that the bar bends. I know that you do that from time to time.
  • Only standard Olympic Barbells can be tracked.
  • The entire barbell needs to fit in the screen throughout the entire lift. Otherwise the algorithm will fail. Sorry.
  • The barbell should be at least half the width of the screen. So don't film from 50 feet away or something crazy like that.
  • It also can't hurt to film in a well-lit area.

Between fast-twitch and slow-twitch muscle fibers, a one rep max will rely more heavily on your fast-twitch muscles and your motor neurons. Check out why bodybuilders are more jacked than power lifters for more information on this idea. This is why a calculator like this one that relies on a single rep is more effective than the typical one rep max calculators that use a standard regression to determine a one rep max based on the number of repetitions you can perform of a given weight. The one you typically see is something like:

  • max = weight / (1.0278 - (0.0278 * reps))

It is also for this reason that it's preferable to select a weight that is heavy. This should make it easier to use type II fast twitch muscle fibers to mimic a real one rep max as much as possible. Additionally, a lighter weight will result in a much faster rep, leaving less room for observation from the system and more room for error.
